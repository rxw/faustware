title: "On the most important person in history"
date: 2026-04-21

It was a quiet night, the kind where the refrigerator hums a little too loudly, when I found myself watching a video called "Wikipedia's Biggest Mystery." I don't remember how I got there. That's usually how it happens with videos like this - you blink, and suddenly you're somewhere you never intended to go, like a side street that wasn't on the map.

The video was about some researchers at MIT who tried to figure out the most important person who ever lived by counting how many languages their Wikipedia page had been translated into. The results were strange, the way most true things are strange. First place: Jesus Christ. Second: Barack Obama. Third: a young actor named Corbin Bleu, who once played a supporting role in a movie about a high school musical. Somewhere out there, the theory goes, a single dedicated editor had quietly translated his biography into forty languages, for reasons no one will ever fully understand. People do things like that. They fall into obsessions the way you fall into a dream, without meaning to, and without an exit.

I made myself a cup of coffee and thought about a class I took in college, a long time ago now, called "Networks." It was taught by the economics department, which never made much sense to me, but not much does. We read a paper about the Medici family in Renaissance Florence - not the richest family, not the most powerful on paper, but the one that sat quietly in the middle of everything, the place where all the roads happened to cross. Marriages, loans, favors - they all seemed to pass, sooner or later, through the Medici living room. There's a name for this in network science. Betweenness centrality. I like the phrase. It sounds like something you'd feel at three in the morning, unable to sleep, listening to a record you've heard a hundred times before.

The same basic idea, it turns out, is what made Google into the company it is now. A way of measuring importance by who points toward whom. So I thought: why not do the same thing with Wikipedia itself? Download the whole thing - it's just sitting there, on a server somewhere, patient as a cat - and let a machine read every link between every person who ever lived, and see who rises to the top.

The first list looked like this:

1. Donald Trump
2. Barack Obama
3. George W. Bush
4. Adolf Hitler
5. Bill Clinton
6. Ronald Reagan
7. Franklin D. Roosevelt
8. Joe Biden
9. John F. Kennedy
10. Richard Nixon

And a second, simpler method gave this:

1. Donald Trump
2. Barack Obama
3. Pope John Paul II
4. George W. Bush
5. Pope Francis
6. Bill Clinton
7. Ronald Reagan
8. Adolf Hitler
9. Joe Biden
10. Elizabeth II

The popes appeared in one list and disappeared from the other, the way certain people drift in and out of your life without explanation. It made a kind of sense, once I thought about it. The presidents kept linking to each other, endlessly, like men in a room full of mirrors, each one convinced he was looking out a window. And all of this, I should say, was only English Wikipedia - which meant even the mirrors had an accent.

Both lists suffered from the same quiet flaw: they mostly told you who happened to be alive recently. So I adjusted for that, and something new came up out of the water, like a shape you weren't sure was really there.

1. William Shakespeare
2. Napoleon
3. Abraham Lincoln
4. George Washington
5. Wolfgang Amadeus Mozart
6. Johann Sebastian Bach
7. Adolf Hitler
8. Ludwig van Beethoven
9. Aristotle
10. Charles Darwin

It looked familiar, like the wallpaper of a classroom you sat in as a child. Not wrong, exactly. But it was a list of people we'd simply agreed, over centuries, to keep talking about - not necessarily the people who'd changed the shape of the world the most. Newton wasn't there. Nobody who discovered a vaccine was there. It made me think about how fame and consequence are two different rivers that happen to look alike from a distance, but never actually meet.

So I tried once more, this time counting only the links that came from things - inventions, laws, theories, ideas that had to be caused by somebody before they could exist at all. And the list that came back felt different, quieter, more like something true:

1. Napoleon
2. George Washington
3. Carl Linnaeus
4. Aristotle
5. Alexander the Great
6. William Shakespeare
7. Abraham Lincoln
8. Isaac Newton
9. Charles Darwin
10. Leonardo da Vinci

Carl Linnaeus, in third place. I sat with that for a while. A quiet Swedish botanist who decided how we would name every living thing, forever, without anyone really noticing he'd done it. That's a strange kind of immortality - not being remembered, exactly, but being impossible to get around.

I don't know which list is right. Maybe none of them are. Importance is a slippery thing, like a cat that comes and goes through a gap in the fence, never quite yours, never quite anyone's. But it felt, for a moment there in the quiet of the kitchen, like I'd gotten a little closer to something true. And then the coffee got cold, the way it always does, and I went on with my night.
